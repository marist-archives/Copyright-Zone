About the Marist College Copyright Zone Web Page

Representing an attempt by Congress to provide guidelines for the use of copyright materials in the online education environment, the Technology, Education and Copyright Harmonization (TEACH) Act became law in 2002. Generally, copyright law treats digital and non-digital copyright-protected works similarly. Yet, digital uses, including online learning and course management systems merit special attention. For instance, it is not necessarily true that a given source used legally for an on the ground course can be used legally online.

Online technologies provide a number of options for faculty and students, ranging from fully online courses to a course management system that augments more traditional on the ground courses. In both instances, educational content is provided to students online, thereby raising concerns about copyright infringement. When content is protected by copyright law, the use of the material must be resolved.

The Marist College Copyright Zone web page is here to help. The “Zone” is a resource tool to help guide you in making informed choices about using copyrighted materials safely in the online teaching environment.

How to Play

Marist has divided resources you may want to use into 3 Zones:

  1. The Safety Zone, which contains hundreds of thousands of books, millions of scholarly articles, hundreds of hours of video, and other resources Marist has already obtained copyright permission for you to use.
  2. The Moderately Safe Zone has far fewer resources. For the most part copyright permission has not been obtained by the College and in some cases would require you to obtain permission before they can be used in your class.
  3. The Danger Zone. Resources in this zone definitely require you to obtain permission and pay fees for limited use.

You can click on each zone to link to the materials or external sites that fall within each.

Marist Copyright Policy

It is the policy of Marist College to comply with all existing United States copyright laws including the U.S. Copyright Act of 1976, the TEACH Act of 2002, and the Digital Millennium Copyright Act. All college faculty, staff and students are expected to act both responsibly and ethically when using copyrighted works (such as print and electronic documents, web-based content, visual materials, photographs, music and other forms of creative works) which includes making informed decisions based on the “fair use” exemptions to these laws.

The College strongly recommends that members of the Marist community limit their use of copyright protected materials to the hundreds of thousands of resources available through the James A. Cannavino Library (for which the college already holds the necessary licenses) or the millions of works available for educational use under Creative Commons licenses. If copyright protected materials which are not available through these sources are vital to your work, you may request assistance, through the Library, in seeking the acquisition of, or permission to use these materials. Such requests may take several weeks or months to complete and may include related service fees. It is also possible the desired work is simply unavailable.

If you are unable to find appropriate materials that are already licensed for your use and do not have time to seek permission to use the materials, you can, at your own risk, consider using it under the “fair use” exemption. Decisions to use materials under the “fair use” exemption should be made on a case-by-case basis, re-evaluated each time the materials are used and be documented in writing as means to record the rationale for “fair use”. The College does not assume legal or financial responsibility for any independent application of copyright principles made by College faculty or staff that do not meet the terms of related copyright law or this policy.

What are the Copyright Zones?

Marist’s Copyright Committee is working to identify some of the most common questions faculty have in the very complex world of intellectual property rights. Towards that end it designed a game-based exercise using actions faculty often want to take and putting them into one of three zones: The Safety Zone; The Moderately Safe Zone; or the Danger Zone.

The purpose of the Copyright Zone is to help you understand if what you seek to do is covered by the permissions the College has already obtained.

You can begin by testing your copyright IQ. Drag each example into the appropriate Zone. You may be surprised to learn what you can do “safely,” as well as what types of actions require obtaining permissions and possibly paying licensing fees.

You are also encouraged to submit to the Copyright Committee examples similar to those in this game-based exercise that you are not sure can be done within the Safety Zone. Other questions or comments are also welcomed.

This is not a "one and done" game. New examples based on faculty feedback will constantly be added. Further, intellectual property rights are constantly being altered and defined by the courts as content providers get increasingly aggressive in protecting what they view as their intellectual property in the higher education space.

Marist, like most colleges and universities, has a not so hidden message in all of this: Stick to the Safety Zone. The College spends hundreds of thousands of dollars a year obtaining content that includes permissions for faculty to use in their courses. If you cannot find the resource you want to use in the Safety Zone, let a librarian assist you in trying to find an acceptable alternative. If you are determined to operate in the Moderately Safe or the Danger Zone, you increasingly wander into areas of greater legal complexity and possible costs.
